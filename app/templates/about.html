About Nero

An open-source dashboard for monitoring Cyprus reservoir levels in real time.

Our Mission

Nero exists to make Cyprus's water data accessible, understandable, and useful. The island's 17 major dams are the backbone of its water supply, yet the raw data from the Water Development Department is published in a format that is difficult for ordinary citizens to interpret. We transform this data into a clear, visual dashboard that anyone can use — whether you are a farmer planning irrigation, a journalist covering the drought, a policymaker evaluating supply risk, or a resident wondering why your water bill went up.

Water scarcity is the defining environmental challenge of the eastern Mediterranean. Cyprus has been hit by increasingly severe droughts, and climate projections suggest this trend will continue. We believe that transparent, real-time access to water data is a prerequisite for informed public discourse and effective policy-making. Nero is our contribution to that transparency.

Methodology

Nero fetches data from the Water Development Department of Cyprus via their public API at cyprus-water.appspot.com/api. The data is refreshed automatically every six hours. Historical records span from October 2009 to the present day, giving more than 15 years of trend data for each reservoir.

On first launch, Nero performs an initial seed that downloads the full historical dataset. From then on, incremental syncs fetch only new readings. All data is stored locally in a SQLite database and served directly to users — there is no intermediary processing that alters the raw values. Percentage figures represent the ratio of current storage volume to the dam's maximum design capacity as reported by the Water Development Department.

The upstream API occasionally includes data for Agia Marina dam in its statistics endpoint, but Agia Marina is excluded from the individual dam listings. Its volumes are included in system-level totals but the dam does not appear in Nero's grid view, matching the upstream data source's own treatment.

Severity Thresholds

Nero uses a three-tier colour system to communicate the urgency of each dam's fill level at a glance. These thresholds are based on operational norms used in Cyprus water management:

  • Critical — below 20% capacity. At this level, evaporation losses accelerate, water quality degrades from sediment concentration, and dams may approach dead storage. Agricultural allocations are typically cut and municipal rationing may be introduced.
  • Warning — 20 to 40% capacity. Reserves are reduced and conservation measures are advised. Desalination plants increase output to compensate. Irrigation allocations may be curtailed in some districts.
  • Healthy — above 40% capacity. Normal operational levels. Full agricultural allocations, no restrictions on domestic supply, sufficient reserves to buffer a dry season.

Data Notes

  • Historical timeseries data contains approximately 133 periodic snapshots (not daily) from 2018 onwards. Daily granularity accumulates from the ongoing six-hour sync cycle.
  • Percentage values are displayed on a 0 to 100% scale. Storage and inflow are measured in million cubic metres (MCM).
  • Year-on-year comparison charts aggregate data by month or year for clearer seasonal pattern visibility.
  • Dam metadata (height, river name, coordinates, construction year) comes from the upstream API and may not reflect recent engineering modifications.

About the Project

Nero is an open-source project built with Python (FastAPI), Jinja2 templates, Chart.js for visualisations, and Leaflet.js for the interactive map. The codebase follows test-driven development practices with over 130 automated tests. The project is maintained by an independent developer based in Cyprus with a background in software engineering and data systems.

The name "Nero" comes from the Greek word for water (νερό), reflecting both the project's focus and its Cypriot roots. The project has no affiliation with the Cyprus government or the Water Development Department — it is an independent civic technology initiative that reuses publicly available data.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often is the data updated?

Every six hours, automatically. The sync runs in the background and fetches the latest readings from the Water Development Department's API. During periods of rapid change (heavy rainfall or drought), you may see updates multiple times per day.

Why do some dams show 0% or very low levels?

Small dams (under 2 MCM capacity) can empty entirely during extended dry periods. This is a physical reality, not a data error. These dams respond dramatically to even modest rainfall and can swing from empty to full within days.

What is MCM?

MCM stands for Million Cubic Metres, the standard unit for measuring reservoir volumes in hydrology. One MCM equals one billion litres — enough to fill roughly 400 Olympic swimming pools.

Is this site affiliated with the Cyprus government?

No. Nero is an independent project that reuses publicly available open data. The Water Development Department publishes reservoir readings under a Creative Commons licence, which permits reuse with attribution.

Can I use this data for research or journalism?

Yes. The underlying data is published under CC BY 2.0. You are free to cite, reproduce, or build upon it with appropriate attribution to the Water Development Department and Nero. If you are working on a story or paper, feel free to link to individual dam pages for live references.

How does Cyprus get its drinking water?

Cyprus relies on a combination of dam-stored surface water, desalinated seawater, and groundwater. Desalination plants now produce roughly 70% of the island's domestic drinking water, but dams remain critical for agriculture and as a cost-effective supplement to desalination. When dam levels are high, less desalination is needed, reducing energy costs and carbon emissions.
